Background

Jonas Yoder, Wallace Miller and Adin Yutzy, were three Amish parents who would not let their children, Frieda Yoder, Barbara Miller and Vernon Yutzy, go to public school due to religious reasons, because high school teaches things that usually go against their religion. The Amish also typically learn trades like agriculture or craftsmanship around the age of 14-15 through apprenticeship, which is generally around 8th grade. In the case of these children both Frieda Yoder and Barbara Miller were 15, and Vernon Yutzy was 14, although the legal minimum to drop out of school was 16, which caused all three parents to be fined. Jonas Yoder and the other parents of course did not agree with this and filed a lawsuit against Wisconsin, which lost in multiple courts and appealed every time, until it finally won in the Wisconsin supreme court. This time Wisconsin appealed to the US Supreme Court, which was accepted.

Justices

Chief Justice Warren Burger was selected by President Nixon who although Republican was not heavily partisan, and so he chose Burger who was also conservative but did not show too much partisanship. He was a constructionist and occasionally showed quite a bit of judicial activism in the court which is what Nixon wanted especially when he was trying to build support for the Vietnam war. The Burger Court in general is known as one of the last liberal courts, and was the transitional court between the heavy liberalism in the Warren court to the heavy conservatism in the Rehnquist court.

Decision

The court voted unanimously in favor of Yoder, and in favor of allowing Amish Chidlren to leave school after 8th grade for religious reasons.
Justice Warren Burger wrote the opinion of the court which stated under the Free Exercise Clause of the First Amendment, requiring children to attend school past 8th grade went against the parents rights to raise their children in their preferred religious manner.

The conclusion is inescapable that secondary schooling … contravenes the basic religious tenets and practice of the Amish faith, both as to the parent and the child.

In this case both Justices Lewis Powell and William Rehnquist did not decide on the case leaving it as a unanimous decision.
Justice Byron White wrote a concurring opinion and joined the majority in his vote. He wrote that just the ninth and tenth grades didn’t amount to the importance of the Amish religious practices. He also wrote that if the children who were removed from school did not oppose it as well, then the government has no right to stop them from leaving.

say that the State's interest in requiring two more years of compulsory education in the ninth and tenth grades outweighs the importance of the concededly sincere Amish religious practice to the survival of that sect

Byron White in Wisconsin v. Yoder Concurring Opinion

Justice William O. Douglas technically voted with the majority but did dissent in part specifically for the part where the right to the children's freedom of religion is entirely given to the parents, and not the children. Although this is significant since this could very well happen in cases in the future, it isn’t necessary for this case since the children did admit to leave school purely for their religion not due their parents. Justice White pointed this out, and it is the reason why he concurred, rather than dissented in part with Justice Douglas.

Q. So I take it then, Frieda, the only reason you
are not going to school, and did not go to school
since last September, is because of your religion?
A. Yes.
Q. That is the only reason?
A. Yes. (Emphasis supplied.)

Clauses

Free Exercise Clause of the First Amendment

Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof

Effects & Today

Since the ruling for Wisconsin v. Yoder there have been no cases overturning it, and has been the precedent ensuring protection from the government for parents to teach their kids privately or through alternate methods rather than the public or private school systems.
