Background

On December 16, 1965, John Tinker, Mary Beth Tinker, Hope Tinker, and Paul Tinker along with Christiphor Eckhardt, wore black armbands to school with peace symbols on them to protest against the war. They are told by faculty that the school will not allow them to wear them, but they continue to wear them, in which the principal responds by suspending them all until they return without the armbands.

The Tinkers and Christiphor Eckhardt are contacted by the ACLU and are supported by them to sue the school for violating their first amendment rights. They lose the case in the lower court, and appeal, in which they are met with a split decision, and so they appeal again to the U.S. Supreme Court.

Justices

Earl Warren was Republican but was very bipartisan, often supporting liberal points and conservative ones. Warren's court is known to be one of the most liberal supreme courts. The heavy liberalism in the Warren court is part of why this case did not have a similar ruling to the Schenck v. United States case which put nationalism and support for the war over certain constitutional rights.

Decision

The court voted 7-2 in favor of Tinker, selectively incorporating the freedom of protest for the states.
Justice Abe Fortas delivered the majority opinion, writing that the Tinker family and Christiphor Eckhardt were allowed to protest in school against the Vietnam War, overruling the decision made by the lower court. They decided this way since the students in question did not disrupt other students or interfere with the schoolâ€™s work.

The school officials banned and sought to punish petitioners for a silent, passive expression of opinion, unaccompanied by any disorder or disturbance on the part of petitioners. There is here no evidence whatever of petitioners' interference, actual or nascent, with the schools' work or of collision with the rights of other students to be secure and to be let alone

Justice Potter Stewart concurred, writing that children are not given all of the First Amendment rights. He believes that the rights of the first amendment are for adults and do not entirely include children. He still chose to agree with the opinion of the court in the end.

I cannot share the Court's uncritical assumption that, school discipline aside, the First Amendment rights of children are co-extensive with those of adults

Potter Stewart in Tinker v. Des Moines Concurring Opinion

Justice Byron White wrote a separate short concurring opinion, writing less than a page, in where he notes that there is a difference between communicating by speaking, and committing an act, and that the court should take note of that while deciding the majority opinion.

I deem it appropriate to note, first, that the Court continues to recognize a distinction between communicating by words and communicating by acts

Justice Hugo L. Black dissented, writing that The first amendment does not give people the right to express their opinions at any time and uses a precedent case to support that. In this case specifically the armbands may have distracted other students and school officials, and so the school was legally allowed to discipline the students. Justice Harlan wrote a much shorter dissenting opinion. He argued that the school officials do have authority over the students, and so they can deal with them accordingly to keep order in the school.

I have never believed that any person has a right to give speeches or engage in demonstrations where he pleases and when he pleases. This Court has already rejected such a notion. In Cox v. Louisiana

Hugo L. Black in Tinker v. Des Moines Dissenting Opinion

Clauses

Freedom of Protest in the First Amendment

Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances.

Effects & Today

This case did not overturn any precedents although it did expand on the rights that were ensured by West Virginia State Board of Education v. Barnette, which stated that children had constitutional protection at school.
The Tinker v. Des Moines case set a precedent that is still in effect today, and is cited by several other major supreme court cases, including Hazelwood v. Kuhlmeier and Morse v. Frederick. The ruling from this case still prevents schools from punishing students for protesting, which is why public protests like the mass school walkouts after the uvalde shooting were legal.
