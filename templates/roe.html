Roe v. Wade

7 - 2

Is the right for a woman to get an abortion constitutionally protected?

Background

A woman under the pseudonym Jane Roe (later revealed to be Norma McCorvey) sued Henry Wade, the district attorney of Dallas county, Texas, where she had attempted to have an abortion. The law in Dallas country said that abortions would be deemed illegal unless it is to save the mother’s life. Jane Roe Appealed from the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Texas to the Supreme Court of the United States, claiming that her right to have an abortion came from the right to personal privacy established by the First, Fourth, Fifth, Ninth, and Fourteenth Amendments.

Justices

Chief Justice Warren Burger was conservative but did not show too much partisanship, generally deciding conservatively in more cases but also liberally quite a bit. Justice Burger’s court was liberal which was the reason for why this case was decided in favor of abortion.

Decision

The court voted 7-2 in partially favor of Roe, and abortion. The decision they reached did not entirely legalize abortion, but did prevent states from prohibiting them completely.
Justice Harry A. Blackmun writes the majority opinion for the court, and explains how the decision will regulate abortion. He uses mortality rates of the trimesters of pregnancy to value how far regulation can go. For the first trimester abortion cannot be regulated by the state, and it is entirely up to the doctor and the mother. For the second trimester the state may place “reasonable” regulations on abortion related to the health of the mother. During the third trimester the state may regulate abortion as much as needed as long as exceptions are made for when the mother’s life is at risk.

Clauses

The 14th Amendment

The 14th Amendment incorporates the right to privacy collected from several other Amendments to state laws, and ensures that the people’s rights are protected both federally and on the state level.

Effects & Today

Roe v. Wade case has now been overturned by the Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization case which left the decision of whether abortion should be regulated or not entirely up to the hands of the states.
